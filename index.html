Defining the Stack for IoT

If you are responsible for bringing “Internet of Things” products to market, you need to separate the IoT hype from reality. The IoT vision is vast, but there’s little consensus around best practices and common tech stacks. Most of today’s Internet of Things projects are highly custom, brittle, and IoT project teams are forced to choose between proprietary platforms or re-invent the wheel each time.

Participate with technology thought leaders with the goal of defining the IoT technology stack and the interoperability requirements that will help to drive IoT forward. If you want to collaborate with hands-on people solving real IoT challenges, IOT Stream Conf is the place to be.

Moving from Platforms to Stacks

Just as early Web and Mobile development was highly custom, today’s IoT projects don’t follow common practices or leverage a common stack. Today’s IoT teams must either select proprietary platform players or reinvent security, communication, analytics and more for each project. IoT Stream Conf will discuss and solicit opinions about each layer in the IoT stack and the “best-of-breed” products and services that address each of these layers. The overall goal is to help drive the IoT infrastructure market to a rich ecosystem of interoperable commercial and open source options addresses all layers of the IoT Stack.

IoT Layer: Data Stream Network and Connectivity

The first word of “IoT” is “Internet”, and yet there’s no consensus around how embedded devices should communicate. This track addresses the variety of Internet communication needs (security, uni/bi-directional, occasional vs. real-time streaming, 1:1, 1:many, etc.) and the various options to solve these communication needs. This track discusses vendors and open source options that address this layer of the stack, protocols, interoperability with other layers, and more. Also discussed is what computation can be done “on the edge” to reduce bandwidth and server-side computation load.

IoT Layer: Big Data, Analytics, and Triggers

The big promise of the IoT is to leverage the massive data collected into real-time visibility, intelligence, and action. Today, IoT project teams are presented with a myriad of vertical solutions, no-SQL databases, analytics products, and more. The track will focus on products, solutions, and custom development happening in this area, and techniques for ingesting and coping with massive data streams. Discussions will also focus on best practices, protocols, and techniques for integrating the emerging IoT communication layer into the big data layer of the IoT stack.

IoT Layer: Local Area Device Communication and Connectivity

The IoT has generated an explosion of protocols and proposed standards for local area connectivity. BLE, Zigbee, Zwave, Insteon, and 6LoWPAN are just a small sampling of technologies being discussed for local area device connectivity. Further confusing this layer in the stack is the decreasing cost of IP connectivity and the trend toward embedding full TCP stacks on tiny devices. This track will address who’s using these protocols and techniques, experiences (good and bad), and where thought leaders expect things to go.

IoT Layer: Gateways, Standards, and Ecosystems

As more vendors enter the IoT, various industries have begun defining gateways and standards to encourage interoperability and standardization. The last 24 months have seen announcements for AllSeen/AllJoyn, Open Interconnect Consortium, Google Thread, Industrial Internet Consortium, and Apple HomeKit, to name a few. This track will discuss the various differences between these proposed standards, current adoption, and other topics related to interoperability and market timing.

IoT Layer: Connected Devices - Hardware and Chipsets

What innovations in hardware, operating systems, and chipsets are facilitating the IoT? How can new products and frameworks facilitate IoT development for a wider audience of developers that may not be embedded systems developers? What are the best, most cost-effective chipsets for Internet connectivity, and how should IoT architects evaluate their options? This track will address these questions and showcase some of the latest designs that are enabling IoT products that don’t require massive knowledge of embedded systems programming models.

An open source, reference data infrastructure for IoT Sujee Maniam
- Adriatic Room
We are already in the era of ‘connected things’ -- our mobile phones, computers, tablets and wearables sync and talk via ‘cloud’. It is not hard to imagine the next wave as more and more devices are connected (cars, thermostats ...etc). This is what is called as ‘Internet of Things’ (IoT).

IoT presents a lots of challenges; especially data coming in at high volume and high velocity -- imagine billions of devices each sending readings every few seconds or minutes. All this data need to be analyzed pretty quickly. Plus we need to think about security, privacy and compliance.

In this talk we will present a reference architecture for IoT. We draw on industry’s best practices on Big Data including Lambda architecture patterns. Lambda architecture outlines generic, scalable and fault-tolerant data processing. We will illustrate a potential data pipeline and discuss how each stage might be implemented and technology choices at each stage. The talk will also highlight up and coming Big Data technologies that may play a key role in IoT infrastructures.

Harnessing microservices and composable services for agility and scale in IoT applications Chad Arimura, Iron.io
- Adriatic Room
Fast-moving, agile organizations such as Netflix, Gilt and Untappd are embracing microservices as the new foundation for software development - a direct response to more costly and cumbersome monolithic approaches of the past. A composable services architecture breaks application development into discrete, logical tasks that are better suited for handling event-driven workloads within distributed cloud environments. The microservices approach yields:
  • Improved scalability through matching event loads to resource utilization
  • Increased agility with more flexible computing environments
  • Simplified migration from legacy architectures into more modern distributed infrastructures
This session will review the best practices for developers who must address the challenges of deploying and managing service-driven architectures for IoT and stream-oriented workloads.
