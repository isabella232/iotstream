On Community Bryan Hughes

Getting involved in a developer community can be highly rewarding, but entails risk as well. When we speak at conferences, give a demo, or even just attend an event, we make ourselves vulnerable. We open ourselves for criticism, rejection, and sometimes harassment. We don't think we're qualified to be here, we don't think we'll be accepted socially. So what can we do? Making our community a safe and welcoming place is the responsibility of everyone involved, not just the organizers. Bryan will talk about his own journey into the NodeBots community, and some of the things he has learned about community over the years.

Bryan Hughes is a front-end engineer at Rdio, and an organizer for the NodeBots SF meetup group, and a somewhat regular speaker at conferences. In his spare time he likes to do robotics work with the Raspberry Pi and JavaScript, having added support for the Raspberry Pi to the Johnny-Five robotics library.

Being Effective in a Virtual World Diane Tate

Open source started it and the world is following" can describe many phenomena; it certainly describes remote working. What lessons can we learn from Mozilla when it comes to wrangling groups of contributors across geographies, cultures, skill sets and a number of other diverse characteristics?

Diane has worked with developers in a variety of capacities: as a recruiter, a product manager at Schwab and for various startups, and as program manager at Mozilla building out both its developer startup program, WebFWD as well as wrangling the developer relations team. She appreciates her video camera and its ability to bridge gaps of time and space and wants to take the "hate" out of the common love-hate relationship many have with remote working.

Test-driven client-side apps Pete Hodgson

Writing high quality, maintainable client-side code is becoming increasingly important as we continue to move more logic into the browser. We should treat our client-side code with the respect it deserves.

In this talk I'll show how we can use unit-testing to make our JavaScript apps better. We'll briefly cover the fundamentals of unit-testing and what tools are available for testing client-side code. We'll then dive into real-world examples, looking at how to unit-test a simple backbone app, test-first. We'll see how testing drives us towards cleaner and more maintainable code, with clear separation between the DOM and your application logic. Along the way we'll also learn a few JavaScript testing protips(tm).

Pete is a Lead Consultant at ThoughtWorks. He helps teams get more awesome at delivering maintainable software at a sustainable pace, using agile practices like test-driven design, pairing and continuous delivery. After horrible experiences with JavaScript in the early 2000s Pete saw the light and fell in love with this quirky little language while leading a team building a pure-JavaScript single-page mobile banking application. He's also a fan of functional programming, dynamic languages, hypermedia API design, and brewing his own beer.

Web Security in Node.js Applications Scott Smith

"With billions of people using the Internet, the potential for nefarious or outright criminal users hitting your site is very high. With attacks ranging from MITM, CSRF, Script Injection, Clickjacking to name a few, it is imperative that we as developers understand these exploits, how they work, how they can be stopped, and how to implement the code or setup to do so. As developers, we tend to focus on the core of what our application does. Unfortunately, security tends to be overlooked or at best an afterthought.

In this talk we will explore the OWASP top 10 web application security flaws including how they work and best practices to protect your application from them. The focus will be on Express web applications in Node, but the principles shown can be applied to any framework or environment."

Scott Smith is a VP of Product Development during the day and full stack Node and .NET developer by night. Most recently Scott created Coderbits, an intelligent portfolio site for developers and designers, and sold it to Appirio to power their Top Coder platform. He has a passion for REST APIs and loves sharing that passion with others. When not working, you will probably find him SCUBA diving on a tropical island.
